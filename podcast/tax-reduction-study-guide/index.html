Chapter 02

The Advisor Problem

Robert argues that the vast majority of retail investors are unknowingly in a closet index fund — their portfolio is dressed up as sophisticated but simply tracks the market up and down, all while paying advisory fees. Understanding this is the first step to demanding more.

The Harsh Reality

"Nine out of ten individuals — your advisor tells you it's spruced up, it's custom, it's this, it's that — but at the end of the day it just simply tracks the market up and down. And then you pay a fee for that."

Correlation measures how closely your portfolio moves relative to a benchmark index (like the S&P 500). A correlation of:

  • 1.0 = Your portfolio moves perfectly with the index (up when it's up, down when it's down)
  • 0.0 = No relationship to the index
  • -1.0 = Moves opposite to the index

Robert finds that most client portfolios he reviews have a correlation "darn near one" — meaning they're paying fees for what amounts to an expensive index fund.

The Math Problem

If your correlation is 1.0 and the market drops 30%, you drop ~30%. If your advisor charges 1%, you're paying to experience the full market drawdown. You could just buy a Vanguard index fund for near zero cost.

Robert reveals a systemic conflict he experienced firsthand at a major brokerage firm:

  • Advisors typically earn fees based on assets under management (AUM)
  • At his previous firm, if money sat in cash for more than 3 months, the advisor stopped earning their management fee on it
  • This created an incentive to always be invested — regardless of market conditions
  • The decision of when to reallocate was driven by the advisor's pay, not the client's best interest

This is why Robert runs KCP as an independent fiduciary — legally bound to put client interests first, removing fee-based incentive conflicts.

Chapter 03

Structured Notes (Bank Notes)

Structured notes — also called bank notes — are one of KCP's primary portfolio tools. They're issued by the world's largest banks and offer defined, predictable returns with significant downside protection. Robert sees them as a way to bring institutional strategies to retail investors.

12%+
Target annual coupon on KCP structured notes
30%
Downside buffer before you stop earning the coupon
Monthly
Coupon payment frequency on KCP-preferred notes

A structured note is a debt instrument issued by a large bank (JP Morgan, Morgan Stanley, BNP, etc.) with a return linked to the performance of one or more underlying indexes.

KCP's preferred structure:

  • Tied to 2–3 indexes (e.g., S&P 500, Russell 2000, KRE Regional Bank Index)
  • Pays a monthly coupon (targeting 12%+ annually)
  • Has a 30% downside buffer — as long as none of the tracked indexes fall more than 30%, the coupon is paid
  • If any index falls more than 30%, the coupon stops — but you don't lose principal unless the index is below the buffer at maturity
Real Example from the Episode

Robert mentioned a Morgan Stanley note that priced at 12.30%, tied to the S&P 500, Russell 2000, and KRE Regional Bank Index, with 30% downside protection — meaning all three indexes would need to fall more than 30% before the coupon is lost.

Structured notes are not FDIC insured — they're backed by the creditworthiness of the issuing bank. If the bank fails, the note could be worthless. This is why Robert focuses exclusively on the world's largest, highest-rated banks.

In the episode, Robert addressed a claim from a competitor that BNP (one of their note issuers) had "been in bankruptcy since 2019." He called this flat-out false — BNP carries an A+ credit rating, higher than JP Morgan, and its credit default swap pricing (the cost to insure against default) is among the lowest in the world.

Key Point

Credit default swaps (CDS) are a market-based measure of default risk. The lower the CDS spread, the less the market believes a bank will default. BNP's CDS is among the lowest globally.

Chapter 04

Principal Protection: The Indexed Annuity

Not all annuities are bad. Robert breaks down a specific 5-year indexed annuity he views as a legitimate bond alternative — one with zero downside risk, locked-in gains, and market-linked upside up to a cap. Understanding the difference between this and the income annuities most advisors sell is crucial.

The Nuance

"You can't tell me we're against annuities. We're just against the bulk of annuities. Out of 100 different annuity strategies, there's really only one or two that we would use."

The KCP-recommended indexed annuity works like this:

  • Tied to an index (typically S&P 500) — pure price movement, no income component
  • Annual cap: Currently 10–11% — you earn 100% of the index's gain, up to the cap
  • Zero floor: If the S&P is negative — even down 50% — your return is 0%, not negative
  • Locked-in gains: Each year's positive return is locked in permanently — you never give it back
  • 5-year term: Popular for IRA money
  • No fee to investor: Commission is paid by the insurance company, not the client

Income annuities are widely sold with pitches like "7% guaranteed income for life." Robert's critique:

  • The "7% guaranteed" refers to a growth rate on an income account — not your actual investable cash value
  • Insurance companies know actuarially that most accounts won't go to zero — so they limit withdrawals and growth just enough to stay solvent
  • Inflation risk: A fixed income stream loses purchasing power over 20–30 years of retirement
  • Most clients don't actually understand what they own
The Contrast

The pure growth indexed annuity Robert recommends has no income component — it's simply a principal-protected growth vehicle. This is fundamentally different from the income annuities typically pitched by financial advisors.

Tax Reduction: The Year-Round Strategy

Tax planning isn't a November–December scramble — it's a 12-month discipline. KCP's energy fund strategy allows accredited investors to dramatically reduce taxable income while maintaining investment distributions. This is the episode's central thesis.

~$90K

~20%
Targeted annual distributions at $65/barrel oil
$1M+
Net worth required to qualify as an accredited investor

The strategy uses Intangible Drilling Cost (IDC) deductions available in the U.S. tax code for oil and gas investments:

  • Investors in the KCP energy fund can deduct a large portion of their investment as a tax deduction against active or passive income
  • In 2023, the deduction was ~$89,750 per $100,000 invested
  • Generally the full 100% deduction is achieved over two years (often 50–90% in year one)
  • This is entirely within the U.S. tax code — Robert calls it "very black and white"
  • Additionally, investors receive targeted distributions of ~20% annually (at $65/barrel oil)
Practical Use Case

If you sold a business, exercised stock options, have significant capital gains, or are doing a Roth conversion — the energy strategy can offset the resulting taxable income. Instead of paying taxes on a large income event, you invest in the fund and generate a deduction of approximately equal size.

Who qualifies (Accredited Investor):

  • Net worth of $1M+ not including your primary residence, OR
  • Annual income of $200K+ (individual) or $300K+ (joint) for the past two years

Best use cases: Business sale, building sale, stock options, high W-2 income, capital gains, Roth IRA conversion

Important disclaimers Robert emphasizes:

  • KCP is not a tax advisor — all tax-related discussion is for informational purposes only
  • Always consult your CPA or tax professional before making decisions
  • Distributions are targeted — actual results depend on oil prices and fund performance
  • Energy investments are illiquid and involve commodity risk
Why You Haven't Heard This Before

Robert notes that big banks and brokerage firms don't allow their advisors to offer energy fund strategies — creating a structural gap that only independent advisors can fill.

Chapter 06

Portfolio Construction & The Hedge Principle

Robert's portfolio philosophy is deceptively simple: lose less, make more. The compounding math of limiting drawdowns outperforms any attempt to time the market or chase returns. The key is building a real hedge — not just hoping the market recovers.

The Core Principle

"I'm not a math genius, but I know that if I lose less, I make more. Really simple philosophy. I lose less, I make more."

Robert addresses the large group of investors sitting in cash, waiting for markets to drop before investing. His data:

  • Historically, stocks have a 75% probability of closing lower at some point in the future — which sounds like good odds for waiting
  • But that means 25% of the time, stocks just keep running and the correction never gives you a better entry point
  • Timing the market is essentially a coin flip — not an edge

The solution: rather than making macro calls, build a portfolio that doesn't require perfect timing. Use structured notes with buffers, indexed annuities with floors, and hedged strategies so you can stay invested with protection.

Robert advocates for momentum-based, algorithm-driven investment strategies as part of a diversified portfolio:

  • These strategies don't care about headlines, macroeconomic predictions, or gut feelings — they follow mathematical signals
  • Written by data scientists and PhDs, backtested extensively
  • KCP's "Growth Plus" model (which they access as a conduit): 700%+ return since 2015 vs. the S&P's ~320% over the same period
  • Appropriate allocation: 5–15% of portfolio, not majority (due to volatility)
